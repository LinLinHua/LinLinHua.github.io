This work was conducted during my time as a Research Assistant with the INHALE team at Imperial College London, funded by the EPSRC grant EP/T003189/1. I co-authored the paper "Multi-Output Regression with Generative Adversarial Networks (MOR-GANs)," published in *Applied Sciences* in 2022 [1]. My primary contribution was the software implementation in Python, TensorFlow, and Keras, and the comparative benchmarking against baseline architectures.

## Background: The Problem with Standard Regression on Multi-Modal Data

Regression — finding the relationship between input and output variables — is one of the most fundamental tasks in data analysis. Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) is the standard non-parametric method for small, noisy datasets, based on Bayesian inference over a distribution of functions [2]. It is principled, well-understood, and performs well on many real-world problems.

GPR has a structural weakness, however: it predicts a **single-valued posterior mean** for each input. When the true relationship between input and output is multi-modal — when a single input $x$ can legitimately correspond to multiple valid output values — GPR averages across the modes and predicts the empty space between them.

The canonical example is the annulus: a dataset of points uniformly distributed within a ring. Given an x-coordinate at the ring's edge, there are two valid y-values (top and bottom of the ring). GPR, forced to produce a point estimate, predicts the center of the ring — a region containing no data at all. This failure mode is not a deficiency of any particular GPR implementation; it is a consequence of the model's foundational assumptions.

Real biological and environmental data frequently has this structure. The motivating application in this project was modeling the effect of silver nanoparticles on airway smooth muscle cells — the same nanoparticle concentration and exposure time could produce different responses across different cell donors, different experimental runs, and different measurement conditions. Forcing a point estimate onto this inherently variable data loses the distributional information that matters most [3].

GANs, introduced by Goodfellow et al. in 2014, are generative models that learn to map a simple noise distribution to a complex data distribution by training a generator and discriminator in an adversarial game [4]. Wasserstein GANs (WGANs), proposed by Arjovsky et al. in 2017, improve training stability by replacing the Jensen-Shannon divergence with the Earth Mover's distance, and enforce the required Lipschitz constraint via a gradient penalty term [5, 6]:

$$L = \mathbb{E}_{\tilde{x}\sim P_g}[D(\tilde{x})] - \mathbb{E}_{x\sim P_r}[D(x)] + \lambda\,\mathbb{E}_{\hat{x}\sim P_{\hat{x}}}\left[(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}}D(\hat{x})\|_2 - 1)^2\right]$$

The key insight of MOR-GAN is architectural. A standard conditional model learns $p(y \mid x)$ — it is forced to produce a single output for each input. The WGAN generator instead learns the full joint distribution $p(x, y)$, producing both variables together as its output. Because the generator never had to commit to a single $y$ for a given $x$, it inherently captures multi-modal relationships.

Prediction uses back-propagation through the trained generator (Algorithm 1 in the paper): given a target $x$ value, the algorithm searches the noise input space for all latent vectors that produce samples near $x$, then reads off the corresponding $y$ values — recovering the full multi-modal distribution of $y$ at that point.

## What We Did

My role on this project was the software implementation of the WGAN architecture and the comparative benchmarking.

I implemented the WGAN Generator and Critic networks in Python using TensorFlow and Keras. The generator is a four-layer MLP taking Gaussian noise as input and outputting joint $(x, y)$ samples; the critic is a four-layer network with layer normalization and dropout to prevent overfitting. For the multi-output (MOR-GAN) extension, I adapted the architecture to use convolutional layers, replacing the fully-connected structure to handle multi-dimensional outputs.

I ran comparative benchmarking of the WGAN model against Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), AutoEncoders (AEs), Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), and Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) across a suite of synthetic datasets with varying properties: uni-modal and multi-modal distributions, single- and multi-output regression, 1D and 2D inputs, with and without additive Gaussian noise.

Statistical validation used the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test for 1D datasets and the Mann-Whitney U test for the silver nanoparticle biological data, computing average p-values between the generated and real distributions to quantify how well the model learned the true data distribution.

The central result was that MOR-GAN successfully learned multi-modal distributions — including the annulus and co-varying spiral datasets — where GPR failed entirely. On the silver nanoparticle biological data, MOR-GAN performed comparably to GPR on well-sampled conditions and better on conditions with high inter-donor variability, where distributional modeling is most valuable.
