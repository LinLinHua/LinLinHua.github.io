This post examines the sidewall chemistry of two competing TSV etch methods — Bosch (cycled) and scallop-free — through the lens of a 2014 ULVAC study that connected XPS surface analysis to downstream reliability concerns. During my time as a Process Engineer at Lam Research, I worked directly with DRIE tools and Cu integration flows, so the tradeoffs described here were ones I encountered in production.

## Background: Why TSV Sidewall Quality Matters

Through-Silicon Via (TSV) technology is the structural foundation of 2.5D silicon interposers and 3D-stacked devices — it is what allows multiple dies to communicate vertically through the Si substrate rather than through long PCB traces. The value proposition is compelling: shorter interconnects, lower power, higher bandwidth density.

The reliability problem, however, is non-trivial. A TSV is essentially a Cu-filled cylinder drilled through silicon, surrounded by a dielectric liner (typically SiO₂ or SiN), a barrier metal (Ta or TaN), and then the Cu fill. The quality of that stack depends critically on the condition of the etched Si sidewall it sits on. Defects, contamination, or roughness on the sidewall propagate through every subsequent deposition step — and in a capacitor structure embedded in an interposer (Fig. 1), they directly influence electrical leakage and long-term reliability.

## Two Etch Approaches

Deep reactive ion etching (DRIE) of silicon is dominated by two process families:

The **Bosch (cycled) process** alternates between an SF₆ plasma etch step and a C₄F₈ passivation step, producing anisotropic etching with periodic scallop undulations and a fluorocarbon film on the sidewall. The **scallop-free (non-cycle) process** uses SF₆/O₂ without a separate passivation gas, producing a smooth, chemically cleaner sidewall.

## XPS Sidewall Analysis

The central experiment in Murayama et al. used XPS (X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy) to probe the chemical composition of the etched sidewall at three depths — top (10 µm), middle (25 µm), and bottom (40 µm) — for both etch methods (Fig. 5).

The survey spectra (Fig. 6) showed a stark difference: the cycled sidewall has prominent C1s and F1s peaks throughout, while the scallop-free sidewall shows only Si and O — consistent with a clean SiOₓ passivation layer.

The C1s narrow scan for the cycled process (Fig. 7) showed a classic Teflon-like signature: -CF, -CF₂, -CF₃ peaks consistent with residual C₄F₈-derived fluorocarbon polymer. This is not adventitious carbon contamination — the binding energies are 2–4 eV higher than the 285 eV C-C reference.

Figure 9 in the paper quantifies the difference directly. The F1s/Si2p ratio (a normalized measure of surface fluorine concentration) was plotted on a log scale vs. etching depth for both methods.

The gap is approximately **100×** at the top and remains ~50× at 40 µm depth. Fluorine in the scallop-free case is essentially at the detection limit throughout.

## Reliability Implications

Why does residual fluorine matter? Two mechanisms:

**Capacitor dielectric degradation.** The embedded capacitor in a Si interposer uses the TSV trench sidewall as part of the electrode structure. A fluorocarbon layer between Si and the ALD dielectric introduces interface states, increases leakage current, and reduces breakdown voltage. The paper was motivated specifically by this failure mode.

**Cu barrier adhesion.** For TSV fill, a PVD barrier metal (Ta/TaN) must nucleate and adhere to the Si sidewall. Fluorine and carbon contamination reduce adhesion and can cause delamination under thermal cycling stress — a known failure mode in Cu TSV reliability testing. The scallop-free process was also shown to shorten PVD barrier deposition time, a direct cost benefit.

At Lam Research, the tradeoff between Bosch and scallop-free was a real production decision. Bosch has better process latitude for very high aspect ratios (>20:1) and is more forgiving of loading effects across the wafer. Scallop-free requires tighter pressure and gas flow control to maintain the SiOₓ passivation uniformly, but the sidewall quality and downstream integration benefits are significant for reliability-critical applications.
