This project was conducted at the Electrophysics Lab of National Chiao Tung University (NCTU). The objective was to model the astigmatic transformation of Hermite-Gaussian (HG) laser modes into Laguerre-Gaussian (LG) modes carrying Orbital Angular Momentum (OAM), and to visualize the resulting wave patterns, phase singularities, and 3D particle dynamics in MATLAB.

## Background: Structured Light and Orbital Angular Momentum

Most people picture a laser beam as a simple Gaussian spot. This is the fundamental mode — HG₀₀ in the Hermite-Gaussian notation — the lowest-order solution to the paraxial wave equation in a stable laser resonator. Stable resonators actually support an entire family of transverse electromagnetic (TEM) modes, each with a distinct spatial pattern described by Hermite polynomials in Cartesian coordinates [1].

The Laguerre-Gaussian modes are the cylindrical counterpart. Where HG modes have nodal lines, LG modes have a helical phase structure: the wavefront spirals by $l$ full $2\pi$ cycles for each circuit around the beam axis. At the axis itself, the phase is undefined, the field amplitude is zero, and you have an **optical vortex** — a topological phase defect that carries Orbital Angular Momentum of $l\hbar$ per photon [2].

This was first recognized by Allen et al. in 1992, who showed that LG modes carry well-defined OAM distinct from spin angular momentum (polarization) [2]. The discovery opened a field: OAM beams are now used in optical tweezers for rotating particles, quantum communication using OAM as an information carrier, and stimulated emission depletion (STED) microscopy.

The fundamental Gaussian beam propagates according to:

$$I(r,z) = I_0 \left(\frac{w_0}{w(z)}\right)^2 \exp\left(-\frac{2r^2}{w(z)^2}\right)$$

where the beam radius evolves as $w(z) = w_0\sqrt{1 + (z/z_R)^2}$ and the Rayleigh range is $z_R = \pi w_0^2 / \lambda$. Higher-order LG modes add a radial polynomial factor and the azimuthal phase $e^{il\phi}$, which is the signature of OAM.

The HG and LG mode families are related by a unitary transformation — they are different bases spanning the same space of beam modes. This means there exists a physical optical element that converts between them.

## The Astigmatic Mode Converter

An Astigmatic Mode Converter (AMC) consists of two identical cylindrical lenses separated by $\sqrt{2}$ times their focal length. A cylindrical lens focuses in one transverse direction only, introducing different phase delays for the horizontal and vertical components of the beam [3].

The key effect is the **Gouy phase**: a focused beam acquires an additional $\pi$ phase shift (totaling $\pi/2$ per focus) relative to a plane wave as it passes through a focal point. A cylindrical lens introduces a Gouy phase shift in one direction only. By choosing the lens separation correctly, the AMC introduces a relative $\pi/2$ Gouy phase shift between the orthogonal Cartesian components of an HG mode, converting:

$$\text{HG}_{n,m} \xrightarrow{\text{AMC}} \text{LG}_{p,l}$$

where the radial index $p$ and topological charge $l$ are determined by $n$ and $m$. For example, HG₁₀ (two-lobe pattern) transforms into LG₀,₁ (donut-shaped intensity with $l=1$, a single-charge optical vortex).

## What We Did

Using MATLAB, we implemented the full propagation and transformation model for HG and LG beams.

We first modeled the propagation of higher-order HG modes using the paraxial wave equation, computing the complex field amplitude $E(x, y, z)$ at each point in a 3D spatial grid. We verified that the intensity cross-sections reproduced the expected Hermite polynomial patterns — the characteristic multi-lobe structures of HG₀₁, HG₁₀, HG₁₁, and so on.

We then applied the astigmatic transformation numerically by introducing the appropriate Gouy phase shifts to the orthogonal mode components, synthesizing the output LG mode field. The resulting intensity profiles showed the characteristic donut shape, and the phase maps confirmed the helical $e^{il\phi}$ structure with the phase singularity at the beam center.

With the LG beam fields computed, we modeled the **optical forces** on a dielectric microparticle in the beam's potential well. The gradient force pulls a particle toward the intensity maximum:

$$\mathbf{F}_{grad} \propto \nabla I(\mathbf{r})$$

For a standard Gaussian trap, this draws particles to the beam center. For an LG vortex beam, the intensity maximum is a ring — particles are confined to that ring. The scattering force (proportional to intensity) then drives the particle tangentially around the ring. We computed the resulting 3D trajectories numerically, visualizing the orbital motion of a trapped microparticle as a function of beam power, topological charge $l$, and particle size.

The computed trajectories confirmed stable orbital trapping at the radius of the bright ring, with the orbital angular velocity scaling with topological charge — consistent with the theoretical predictions of optical spanners [4]. This validated the connection between the beam's phase structure and its mechanical action on matter, supporting the lab's ongoing research on structured light-matter interactions.
