This project was conducted at the Electrophysics Lab of National Chiao Tung University (NCTU). The goal was to fabricate Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Capacitor (MOS-C) test structures using HfO₂ as the gate dielectric, deposited via the Sol-Gel spin-coating process, and to characterize the electrical properties of the resulting films through C-V and I-V measurements.

## Background: Why HfO₂?

For over four decades, silicon dioxide (SiO₂) served as the gate dielectric in nearly every CMOS transistor. It can be thermally grown directly on silicon with atomic precision, has excellent insulating properties, and is fully compatible with the CMOS fabrication flow. The reason it was eventually replaced has nothing to do with any flaw in the material itself — it has to do with what happens when you make it four atoms thin.

At the 90 nm process node, Intel's SiO₂ gate oxide reached approximately 1.2 nm — roughly four molecular layers. At that thickness, quantum mechanical tunneling allows electrons to pass directly through the insulator, producing gate leakage currents exceeding 1 A/cm² at 1V [1]. This breaks Dennard scaling: the principle that transistors can shrink while power density stays constant no longer holds when standby leakage grows uncontrollably.

The solution is to replace SiO₂ with a physically thicker film of a material that has a higher dielectric constant — a "High-k" dielectric. The drain current in a MOSFET depends on gate capacitance:

$$I_{ds} = \frac{1}{2} \mu C_{ox} \frac{W}{L} (V_g - V_t)^2 \quad \text{where} \quad C_{ox} = \frac{k\epsilon_0 A}{d}$$

A material with higher $k$ can achieve the same $C_{ox}$ at greater physical thickness $d$, exponentially reducing tunneling leakage. Engineers define **Equivalent Oxide Thickness (EOT)** to compare dielectrics on equal capacitance footing:

$$EOT = t_{HiK} \times \frac{3.9}{k}$$

An HfO₂ film ($k \approx 25$) at 3 nm physical thickness behaves like a 0.47 nm SiO₂ film — but the tunneling barrier is 6× wider.

Not every high-k material qualifies. The requirements are strict: $k > 12$, band gap above 5 eV, conduction band offsets greater than 1 eV on both sides of the Si interface to block carrier injection, and thermodynamic stability against reacting with silicon at process temperatures up to 1000°C [2]. TiO₂ ($k = 80$) and SrTiO₃ ($k = 2000$) fail on band gap. La₂O₃ is hygroscopic. Al₂O₃ has $k = 9$, only marginally better than SiO₂. HfO₂ satisfies all criteria: $k \approx 25$, band gap 5.8 eV, heat of formation 271 kcal/mol (higher than SiO₂'s 218 kcal/mol), and good interface quality with silicon [2, 3]. Intel shipped the first Hf-based HKMG transistors in 2007 at the 45 nm node; every leading-edge CMOS chip since has used HfO₂-based dielectrics [4].

## The MOS Capacitor

The standard test structure for gate dielectric characterization is the MOS-C: a metal gate deposited on the dielectric film, which sits on a doped silicon substrate. This sandwich has three operating regimes depending on gate voltage:

In **accumulation**, majority carriers are drawn to the surface and total capacitance is dominated by $C_{ox}$. As voltage increases toward **depletion**, a carrier-depleted region grows in the silicon, adding a depletion capacitance $C_{dep}$ in series and reducing total capacitance. At sufficient positive voltage (for p-type Si), minority carriers form an **inversion layer** at the surface and a conducting channel appears. The full gate stack capacitance involves three series terms:

$$\frac{1}{C} = \frac{1}{C_{ox}} + \frac{1}{C_D} + \frac{1}{C_{Si}}$$

The C-V sweep traces this accumulation-depletion-inversion transition and reveals everything important about the dielectric: the dielectric constant from the accumulation capacitance, the flat-band voltage $V_{fb}$ (which shifts in the presence of fixed oxide charge or interface traps), and the interface trap density $D_{it}$ from frequency dispersion. The I-V measurement quantifies leakage current density as a function of applied field, with a target below approximately $10^{-2}$ A/cm² at operating voltage.

## What We Did

The fabrication workflow covered the complete process from solution preparation through electrical characterization.

We prepared the HfO₂ sol by dissolving hafnium oxychloride (HfOCl₂·8H₂O) in ethanol and initiating hydrolysis and condensation by slowly adding water and a catalyst. This forms a stable suspension of Hf-O-Hf networked nanoparticles — the "sol." We aged the sol at room temperature to allow viscosity to reach a level suitable for spin-coating.

Silicon (p-type) substrates were cleaned using the standard RCA process to remove organic and metallic contaminants. We then applied HMDS (hexamethyldisilazane) treatment to render the surface hydrophobic. This step is critical: a hydrophilic surface causes the sol to dewet and form non-uniform coverage, while the HMDS-treated surface allows the sol to spread uniformly during spin. The HfO₂ sol was spin-coated at controlled speed and acceleration to target a specific film thickness, followed by a soft bake at 150–200°C to remove residual solvent.

After deposition, we fabricated complete MOS-C test structures by depositing metal gate contacts and performed:

**C-V measurements** at multiple frequencies to extract the dielectric constant $k$, flat-band voltage, and interface trap density. We compared our measured C-V curves to ideal theoretical curves to assess interface quality.

**I-V measurements** to quantify leakage current density across the applied electric field range, and compared against the SiO₂ reference to evaluate the improvement from the high-k dielectric.

The results confirmed that Sol-Gel HfO₂ produces films with dielectric constants in the expected range for HfO₂ and MOS-C devices with characteristic C-V behavior. Film non-uniformity — inherently higher for Sol-Gel than for ALD — was the primary limitation, as expected for a wet-chemistry deposition route. Post-deposition annealing under dilute oxygen was necessary to densify the film and reduce oxygen vacancy defects, consistent with the literature [2, 5].

One challenge encountered throughout was the SiO₂ interlayer that forms at the HfO₂/Si interface during annealing, as oxygen diffuses through grain boundaries of the polycrystalline HfO₂ and reacts with the silicon substrate. Since the interlayer and the HfO₂ act as capacitors in series, this extra low-k layer degrades total capacitance and increases EOT — a fundamental limitation of all high-k integration that the industry continues to work around [3].
